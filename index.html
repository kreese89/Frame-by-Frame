Super Smash Brothers Melee (SSBM) is a fighting game released by Nintendo for the Nintendo Gamecube in 2001. Despite its age, SSBM has maintained an active competitive scene throughout most of its lifetime, with recent years seeing some of the highest attendance numbers for tournaments in its competitive history (this can be attributed to a multitude of factors, such as the release of "The Smash Brothers" Documentary and the game's inclusion into the EVO tournament series, the largest annual fighting game tournament in the world). Competitive SSBM tournaments are double elimination brackets with the majority of "sets" being best of three or best of five. Each game in a set consists of two players (who each can pick a character from a roster of 26 possible characteres) each with four lives ("stocks"), who can pick a stage to play on from a specific set of possibles stages. The use of items and other game-influencing mechanics is prohibited throughout the tournament. Unlike most other 2D fighters that feature a health bar that decreases as the player takes damage from attacks, players reaching their demise when the bar reaches zero, SSBM functions completely opposite: Whether a player crosses the borders of the stage (blast zone) determines the death of the player and players are given a percent counter that increases until reaching a hard cap of 999% making them increasingly more likely to hit the blast zone.

Until recently, the majority of statistics related to competitive SSBM have been "high-level", i.e they have mostly involved studying head-to-head matchups of players, winrates of specific players, and winrates of specific characters. While there is much that can be learned from studying these statistics, there is a lack of analysis of in-game statistics. This is analogous to a lack of statistics such as RBI and Batting Average for competitive Baseball analysis. While winning is important, this is very much a "black box" and does little in the way of explanation.

Recent technology - specifically the SmashSight project, which utilizes neural networks to analyze footage of competitive SSBM gameplay in a frame-by-frame manner - allows for the aforementioned in-game analysis. SmashSight specifically takes in footage of competitive SSBM gameplay and returns a comma seperated table containing frame-by-frame readings of each player's stock count and percentages. A frame is 1/60 of a second so this allows for dissecting a match in the most minute details and for conducting smash analysis that is the first of its kind.

In this project we utilize SmashSight's publicly available data of the majority of the top eight sets from the Genesis 5 tournament. A main topic we wish to explore is how winrate is affected via intermediate statistics that we gather from the data: do these statistics linearly correlate to winrate? Is the correlation nonlinear? Do the correlations change when we look at these statistics with regards to players versus when we look at them with regards to characters? Another interesting and fruitful topic we discuss in this project is how holistic judgements about the game are reflected in this data: how do players with holistically categorized playstyles perform statistically? Can we infer a playstyle from a given set of data points? Does Jigglypuff really die early as many have claimed?

After the frame by frame data has been worked down, the goal is then to develop statistics that are good indicators of a winning performance. While Smashsight's records may seem light at the moment, we are able to extract a surprising number of variables that are functions of percentage and stocks (SmashSight gives the percentages and stock counts for a player at each frame of the match). For example, some things that SmashSight logs nicely for us is what percent players are dying at. This alone tells us how long a players life has been, in addition to being a simultaneous measure of the opponent's performance. The frame aspect of the measure also gives our data an excellent quantification of time elapsed, which provides insight into actual game flow (the transactions that occurred between two players).

The realization of SmashSight's power comes down to 4 base statistics:

  1. Average Death Percent Per Game: The sum of the players death percentages over the number of times they died.
  2. Average Kill Percent Per Game: The sum of the players death percentage over the number of times they killed.
  3. Average Damage Dealt Per Frame
  4. Average Damage Taken Per Frame

and 2 composite statistics:

  1. Stock Score: A players average death percentage over their average kill percent (per game)
  2. Damage Score: A players damage dealt per frame over a players damage taken per frame (per game)

Analysis of these 6 variables on win rate would hopefully provide meaningful insight on what is making the great players great and how quantification of in-game mechanics can be better predictors than record based statistics.

def read_fbf_data(url):
    #Getting the data from the given URL
    data = requests.get(url).text.split('\n')
    #Deleting extraneous rows that are not used 
    del data[0]
    del data[1]
    #rows and cols will be used to make the dataframe carrying our data
    rows = []
    cols = ['frame', 'L_Player_dmg', 'R_Player_dmg', 'L_Player_stocks', 'R_Player_stocks']
    #meta_data contains important information about each game that is not predicate on the given frame
    #for instance, player names, characters chosen, and the name of winner is contained in meta_data
    meta_data = data[0].split(',')
    meta_data = [e for e in meta_data if e != 'NA']
    #ret is the dict that we will return
    ret = {}
    L_Player_name = meta_data[1]
    R_Player_name = meta_data[2]
    L_Player_char = standardize_name(meta_data[3])
    R_Player_char = standardize_name(meta_data[4])
    Stage = meta_data[5]
    Winner = ''
    #checking to see which character is listed as the winner and assigning accordingly
    if meta_data[3] in meta_data[6] :
        Winner = L_Player_name
    else:
        Winner = R_Player_name
    ret['L_Player_Name'] = L_Player_name
    ret['R_Player_Name'] = R_Player_name
    ret['L_Player_Char'] = L_Player_char
    ret['R_Player_Char'] = R_Player_char
    ret['Stage'] = Stage
    ret['Winner'] = Winner
    #this for loop generates the dataframe that will contain the frame-by-frame information of the game
    for i in range(1, len(data)):
        row = []
        frame_data = data[i].split(',')
        frame_data = [e for e in frame_data if e !='NA']
        frame = frame_data[0]
        L_Player_dmg = frame_data[1]
        R_Player_dmg = frame_data[2]
        L_Player_stocks = frame_data[3]
        R_Player_stocks = frame_data[4]
        row = [frame, L_Player_dmg, R_Player_dmg, L_Player_stocks, R_Player_stocks]
        rows.append(row)
        #manually adjusting for an extraneous empty line present in one of the datasets
        if url == 'https://smashsight.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/genesis-5_axe-VS-mango_4.txt' and frame == '4627':
            break
    df_ret = pd.DataFrame(data=rows, columns = cols)
    ret['game_data'] = df_ret
    return ret

#returns the frames on which a given player died provided the frame count and an associated stock count at each frame
#i.e if at frame 461 the player has 4 stocks and at frame 462 the player has 3 stocks,
#the number 461 will be returned (among other frame numbers corresp. to other deaths)
def detect_death(frames, stock_count):
    ret = []
    for fr in range(1, len(frames) - 1):
        if(stock_count[fr] > stock_count[fr + 1]):
            ret.append(fr)
    return ret

#returns the percentages that a player died during a game given the game's frame counter and the associated
#stock and damage counter
def find_death_pct(frames, pcts, stocks):
    ret = []
    death_frames = detect_death(frames, stocks)
    for fr in death_frames:
        curr_frame = fr
        while True:
            if int(pcts[curr_frame]) == -1:
                curr_frame = curr_frame - 1
            else:
                break
        ret.append(pcts[curr_frame])
    return ret
    
#computes the average percents that a player died given a game's frame counter along with the associated
#percentage and stock counts
def compute_avg_death_pct(frames, pcts, stocks):
    avg = 0
    death_pcts = find_death_pct(frames, pcts, stocks)
    for pct in death_pcts:
        avg += pct
    
    avg /= len(death_pcts)  
    return avg
       
#computes the average percent taken by a player over a given range of frames
def compute_avg_dmg_taken(frame, pcts, start_frame, end_frame):
    avg = 0

    uncounted_frames = 0
    
    for fr in range(start_frame, end_frame - 1):
        #frames of invulnerability are represented with -1's in the data
        if pcts[fr] == -1 or pcts[fr + 1] == -1:
            uncounted_frames += 1
            continue
        diff = pcts[fr + 1] - pcts[fr] #damage differential per frame
        avg += diff
        
    #average over the number of frames in the range for which the player CAN take damage
    avg = avg/(end_frame - start_frame - uncounted_frames)
    return avg

#computes the average damage taken by a player over the game 
#specifically, finds the average damage taken for each of the player's stocks and then averages 
#over the amount of stocks they had 
def compute_avg_dmg_taken_per_stock(frames, pcts, stocks):
    death_frames = detect_death(frames, stocks) #getting the frames on which a player died
    avg = 0
    won = False
    #we insert the 0th frame because the functions we use to do the computations require
    #a starting frame and ending frame - put another way, the start frame for each player's
    #first stock is the 0th frame. 
    death_frames.insert(0, 0)
    #In the data, the last frame listed is the frame on which the player who lost lost their 4th stock. 
    #So for a player who lost and dies 4 times, their death_frames will look like [0, f1, f2, f3, f4].
    #d But for a player who won with, say, 1 stock left, their death_frames will look like [0, f1, f2, f3],
    #and so we cannot directly ditect how many frames they spent on their last stock.
    #In this case we simply append the final frame of the game to the end of the array.
    #We then set won to true to make computations work later on
    if len(death_frames) != 4:
        death_frames.append(frames[len(frames) - 1])
        won = True
        
    #this loop will get the average damage taken per frame for each of the players stocks
    for i in range(1, len(death_frames) - 1):
        avg += compute_avg_dmg_taken(frames, pcts, death_frames[i - 1], death_frames[i])
    
    #if the player won or not affects how many extra values we inserted into their death array
    #and so we use this if-else to adjust accordingly
    if won == True:
        avg /= (len(death_frames) - 2)
    else:
        avg /= (len(death_frames) - 1) 
    
    return avg
    
#computes the stock efficiency of the subject player, defined as avg_death_pct/avg_kill_pct
#tar and sub represent target and subject resp. - this distinction is made because 
#of the observation that the percents that player A kills player B is simply the same as 
#the death percents player B, and so we use the single function compute_avg_death_pct to find
#the kill/death pcts
def compute_stock_score(frames, tar_pcts, tar_stocks, sub_pcts, sub_stocks):
    avg_kill_pct = compute_avg_death_pct(frames, tar_pcts, tar_stocks)
    avg_death_pct = compute_avg_death_pct(frames, sub_pcts, sub_stocks)
    return (avg_death_pct/avg_kill_pct)

#computes the damage efficiency of a subject player, defined as avg_dmg_dealt/avg_dmg_taken
def compute_dmg_score(frames, tar_pcts, tar_stocks, sub_pcts, sub_stocks):
    avg_dmg_dealt = compute_avg_dmg_taken_per_stock(frames, tar_pcts, tar_stocks)
    avg_dmg_taken = compute_avg_dmg_taken_per_stock(frames, sub_pcts, sub_stocks)
    return (avg_dmg_dealt/avg_dmg_taken)

There is much future work that can be done using the SmashSight data. One challenge/limitation that we faced with this project was the size of the dataset - while each text file corresponding to each game gives sizeable amounts of frame-by-frame information, there are only a handful of these games provided currently by the SmashSight project. As more tournament games are added to the SmashSight project the results of the analysis done in this project will continue to improve and accuracy and provide much more insight into how these intermediate statistics really do affect winrates across characters and players. Further developments in the technology of SmashSight, such as the addition of data that tracks the position of each character on the screen at each frame, could also allow for exciting new intermediate statistics to be studied - in the case of tracking the position of each character, we could then, for instance, track how often each player controlled the center of the stage during the game, or how effective they were at edge guarding their enemy (making sure the enemy does not get back onto the stage once they are hit off of it). These are all very exciting possibilities, and should usher in a new age of data-science driven analysis of competitive SSBM.
